Everything you need to know as a parent about cyberbullying

Bullying has been a big problem for a long time, and as society began to take it more seriously bullying adapted and changed, and new bullies have invented new methods to bully their victims. Children's and teens' presence on the internet is now more than ever. The internet used to be a safe space for people to find individuals who share the same interests and create friendships. It served as a safe space for people who experience hardships in real life. That safe space for many was broken into by bullying and hate, and suddenly sharing your opinions and interests might potentially lead to you being attacked bullied, and harassed. That safe space ceased to exist. Which can be hard even for social extroverts. Just imagine your 2-3 hours on the internet spent replying to and dodging hate comments and messages.

What is Cyberbullying?

Cyberbullying takes place on online platforms, it can occur via social media, messaging apps, direct texting, chat rooms, and online gaming communities. (What is cyberbullying?, n.d.) Cyberbullying is a repetitive behavior about 30% of people who reported getting bullied online reported getting bullied more than once. (Hinduja J. W., 2019) Cyberbullies aim to cause embarrassment and humiliation for their victims. Cyberbullying includes activities like:

  • Sending, posting, or sharing false, harmful, and negative content about someone.
  • Posting or sharing sensitive information of someone.

It’s prevalence

  • A poll conducted by the UNICEF and the (SRSG) showed that 1/3 of the young people in 30 countries reported being a victim of online bullying. One-fifth reported skipping school due to cyberbullying. (UNICEF poll: More than a third of young people in 30 countries report being a victim of online bullying, 2019 )
  • 59% of teens in the US reported being bullied online (ANDERSON, 2018)
  • A study by McAfee found that 87% of teens observed cyberbullying (Birdsong, 2014)

Effects

Cyberbullying can have different effects on the victims than the “common form of bullying”. If you’re being bullied in school you always know that you can get back home where it’s safe where no one can harm you. On the other hand, with online bullying, victims lose that safe space. You can feel that you’re being attacked in your own house in your room. Cyberbullying affects its victims in different aspects of their lives, it affects kids’ mental health and their social life. Children develop mental health problems such as anxiety and depression. 68% of children currently having mental health problems reported experiencing cyberbullying in the last year. (How can you help, n.d.). Of the students that reported cyberbullying:

  • 25% reported having an experience that led to a face-to-face confrontation with someone.
  • 13% reported being anxious about having to go to school the next day
  • 8% reported having a physical interaction with someone as a result of something that happened on social media.

(Janine M. Zweig, 2013) And these mental health problems can’t be taken as a joke. In some extreme cases cyberbullying victims have committed suicide. That was the case with ateenage girl who unfortunately took her own life after posting a poll asking if she should kill herself and 69% of her” friends” answered yes. And a 13-year-old child who had taken her own life after being bullied by her schoolmates online. All that points to the fact that it does have a major effect on children’s psyche and mental health.

Signs that your child is being bullied online

  • A sudden stop in using electronics
  • Seeming nervous when receiving a text message
  • Withdrawing from social activities
  • Exhibits uneasiness when reminded of going to school or fakes illness to try and skip school
  • Trouble with sleeping
  • Appears unwilling to share his online activity
  • Shows signs of anger or depression especially after getting off his device
  • Quits activities that he/she used to enjoy

How to act as a parent when your child is being bullied online

Finding out that your child is being bullied online can be emotional and painful.

  1. You must show your full support. And make your child feel and be safe.
  2. Engage in a conversation with your child and let him speak and tell what happened freely. Don’t downsize the issue and try to learn as much from him as possible.
  3. Go over the messages and try to collect evidence. And then delete those messages from your child’s device. Block the account and contact.
  4. If the bully is a schoolmate consider notifying the school. Confronting the bully’s parents can be useless because parents usually get defensive when confronted with accusations about their kids.
  5. If your child had suffered severe effects from the bullying consider counseling.
  6. Most importantly keep an open line of communication between you and your kid, to enable your child to come to you early if any future problem arises.

How to protect your children from online bullying

  • Set healthy reasonable tech boundaries. Make clear guidelines and restrictions concerning their tech use. Help your children develop a sense of responsibility for their actions. Developing a healthy manner of using technology.
  • Learn if they have any online friends, and teach them about the difference between real friends and inline friends.
  • Learn about the tech their using, how to use it and stay generally informed. Ask them if needed, children adapt to new technology much faster than adults.
  • Keep an open line of communication
  • Monitor the time they’re spending on their devices
  • Discuss with them what can they share about themselves, and why they should be discrete when it comes to their personal information.
